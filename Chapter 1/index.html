  • The hierarchy of Native American societies ranged from political countries that made war to cultures which were characterized as hunter-gatherers; both societies must be understood in all their complexity to fully understand the American culture.

  • Trade between Natives included food, raw materials, tools, religious artifacts, decorations and diversified diets, improved economies, and created luxury items.
  • Economic trade involved trading fairs. For example, nomadic hunters of the plains exchanged hides and meat with Pueblo farmers for maize, pottery, and cotton.
  • Trade across longer distances included rare and valuable objects such as copper, mica, jasper, obsidians, pipestone, seashells, bear claws, eagle feathers, cloth, iron tools, and weapons.
  • Powerful leaders controlled tribes' wealth but held potlachs to give away their wealth and further assert their authority.

  • Many Native Americans were animists who believed if they pleased the spirits they would have success in their life
  • Women often grew crops and maintained family and by doing so, they believed the spirits blessed them with fertility.
  • Men went through cleaning rituals of hunted animals and by doing so, they believed they allowed for the spirits to properly accept their action.
  • Wars were largely fought as a time for young warriors to prove themselves; many of these wars were more like blood feuds rather than for territorial purposes.

Recall

Empires, Kingdoms, and Ministates

Trans-Saharan and Coastal Trade

Notes

  • Northern Africa was largely connected through civilization and various religions controlling them; on the other hand, it would take until after 1400 for the isolated West and Central Africa to trade with the world.
  • West Africa largely comprises of the Sahel (mostly flat, semiarid), the savanna (grassland with trees, shrubs), and the tropical rain forest and was dominated by Senegal, Gambia, Niger, and Volta (lake in Ghana).
  • Sudanic civilization in East and West Africa started in 9000 BC, moved to the west, developed a style of pottery, made use of cotton, invented techniques for working metals, and had their own monotheistic religion.
  • This Sudanic civilization had 3 major empires: Ghana Empire 800-1200 AD, Mali Empire 1200-1400 AD, and Songhai Empire 1400-later.
  • Gold was largely abundant in Africa and so abundant that when the tenth emperor of Mali made a pilgrimage and spent gold on the way, the gold was devalued for at least 10 years after.
  • In South of West Africa (jungle/savanna), there was a complex set of kingdoms who rose sweet potatoes because cattle would die by tsetse fly (carried parasites).
  • Southwestern African merchants had to travel through all 3 empires and thus the empires had a monopoly over the trans-Saharan trade.
  • Starting in the 1450s, Europeans started to trade in the West Coast of Africa; Mande-speaking states controlled trade, Akan states to the east had goldfields (Gold Coast), and east of Akan was Benin (Slave Coast), Kongo to the South and Duarte Lopez had to negotiate contacts since they had more than 100k residents.
  • Like animists in the Americas and Europe, Africa also believed kings had contact with the spiritual world and it was honored men have a big household and women many children.

Notes

  • Atlantic World consisted of Portugal's interaction with Africans and Spain's interactions with the Americas.
  • Prince Henry of Portugal learned of West Africa's gold supply when speaking to Mediterraneans during the Crusades. He wanted an easy way to get there and his men developed the caravel (sail with triangular sail) to better handle the violent north-African waters. Exchanged salt, wine, and fish for ivory and gold.
  • Henry's efforts of expansion were seconded by Italy—who wanted access to the Indian ocean but didn't want to pass through the agressive Ottoman empire—and they began to finance voyages down Africa.
  • During these trips, they discovered islands which became hotspots for growing new crops such as sugar.
  • Europeans didn't really penetrate Africa because of disease and well-defended kingdoms; but, Europeans established trading posts on the coast.
  • In 1488, Bartolomeu Dias rounded South Africa, and Vasco de Gama reached East Africa in 1497. At first, he was met with Arab power but after returning 5 years later with 21 fighting ships, the Portuguese dominated the Indian Ocean.
  • Slavery in Africa was already common between tribes; Sonni Ali (ruler of Songhai Empire) personally own twelve tribes of slaves.
  • Slavery was also common in trans-Saharan tribe: 9 million Africans sold in trans-Saharan slave trade between 700 and 1900 AD.
  • Initially, Europeans valued gold and commodities but when they realized they could use humans for labor, they started to use slaves in Brazil and America.

  • Later, Columbus colonized the West Indies with 1,000 men but his death went unnoticed in 1506.
  • America get its name for Florentine explorer Amerigo Vespucci who said it wasn't Asia they had landed on.
  • In 1513, Juan Ponce de León explorer Florida and Vasco Núñez de Balboa crossed the Panama (first European to see PO).

  • Spaniards had a special ally: disease. Disease had wiped out millions and destroyed Aztecs' morale.

  • Portugal discovered Brazil and set up sugar plantations by using Native Americans and Africans as slave labor.
